## ISACA'S ROLE AND THE CISA CREDENTIAL (2:30–7:45)

ISACA — the Information Systems Audit and Control Association — was founded in 1969 by a small group of auditors who recognized that the increasing role of computers in business operations demanded specialized audit knowledge. That founding insight remains remarkably relevant. ISACA has since grown into a global professional association serving more than one hundred and seventy thousand members across one hundred and eighty-eight countries. It is the custodian of the CISA, CISM, CRISC, and CGEIT certifications, as well as the COBIT framework, which we will reference extensively throughout this series.

The CISA — Certified Information Systems Auditor — credential was introduced in 1978, making it one of the oldest and most established certifications in the information security and audit space. As of the current examination cycle, more than one hundred and seventy thousand professionals worldwide hold the CISA designation. It is recognized by the United States Department of Defense under Directive 8570, now updated as DoD 8140, as meeting baseline certification requirements for certain Information Assurance workforce categories. In practical terms, this means that for many government contracting roles and a growing number of private-sector positions, CISA is not merely preferred — it is required.

Now, here is something that catches candidates off guard, and ISACA does test on it. The CISA certification is not conferred simply by passing the examination. You must satisfy three distinct requirements. First, you must pass the CISA exam itself. Second, you must demonstrate a minimum of five years of professional information systems auditing, control, or security work experience. Substitutions are permitted — a two-year university degree can substitute for one year of experience, and a four-year degree can substitute for up to two years. A master's degree in information security or a related field can substitute for one year as well, but the maximum total substitution is three years. You must still have at least two years of direct professional experience. Third, you must agree to adhere to the ISACA Code of Professional Ethics and the IS Auditing Standards.

Here is your first exam trap. ISACA periodically tests whether candidates understand that the certification requires both passing the exam and meeting the experience requirement. A question may present a scenario where a candidate has passed the exam but lacks sufficient experience, and the correct answer will be that the individual is not yet CISA-certified. Do not confuse exam passage with certification attainment.

Let me give you a mnemonic for the CISA certification requirements: PEA — Pass the exam, Experience verified, Adherence to ethics. PEA. Simple, durable, and it maps directly to the three gates.

## THE IS AUDIT PROCESS — END TO END (7:45–15:30)

The IS audit process, as defined by ISACA's IS Auditing Standards and Guidelines, follows a structured lifecycle that every CISA candidate must internalize at a granular level. The process is not a checklist — it is a methodology, and the exam will test your ability to determine what action is appropriate at each phase.

The audit process consists of the following phases: planning, fieldwork and execution, reporting, and follow-up. Some frameworks expand this into additional sub-phases, but for CISA exam purposes, these four constitute the canonical lifecycle. Let me walk through each one with the level of detail the exam demands.

Planning is where the audit's trajectory is established. During planning, the auditor defines the audit scope, identifies the audit objectives, assesses risk to determine where to focus effort, and develops the audit program — the detailed procedural roadmap that fieldwork will follow. Planning also includes understanding the auditee's business environment, regulatory landscape, and prior audit history. The CISA exam places extraordinary emphasis on planning. If you encounter a question that asks what the auditor should do "first" in virtually any scenario, the answer almost always involves a planning activity — understanding the environment, reviewing prior findings, or conducting a risk assessment.

Here is a critical exam trap. When the exam asks what an auditor should do "first" or "initially," it is testing whether you understand the sequential logic of the audit process. The correct answer is almost never "begin testing controls" or "issue the audit report." The correct answer is almost always a planning-phase activity. Internalize this pattern.

Fieldwork and execution is where the auditor gathers evidence. This involves performing audit procedures defined in the audit program — examining documents, observing processes, interviewing personnel, re-performing calculations, and testing controls. The auditor is evaluating whether controls are designed adequately and operating effectively. These are two distinct assessments, and the exam will test whether you understand the difference. A control can be well-designed but poorly operated, or it can be operating consistently but inadequately designed for the risk it is meant to address.

Reporting is where audit findings are communicated. The audit report is the auditor's primary deliverable, and it must communicate findings, conclusions, and recommendations in a manner that is clear, complete, and actionable. ISACA standards require that the report be objective and that findings be supported by sufficient, relevant, and reliable evidence. The report is addressed to the appropriate level of management and, in many organizations, to the audit committee of the board of directors.

Follow-up is the phase that many candidates underestimate. After the report is issued and management has responded with corrective action plans, the auditor has a responsibility to monitor whether remediation actually occurs. The CISA exam tests this explicitly. An auditor's job does not end when the report is delivered. The auditor must verify that management has implemented agreed-upon corrective actions within the agreed-upon timeframe. If management accepts the risk rather than remediating a finding, the auditor must ensure that this risk acceptance is documented and that it has been approved by an appropriate level of management.

Let me give you a mnemonic for the audit process phases: PFRF — Plan, Fieldwork, Report, Follow-up. Or think of it as "Professionals Find Real Facts." Each word maps to a phase. Professionals equals Planning. Find equals Fieldwork. Real equals Reporting. Facts equals Follow-up.

## THE AUDIT UNIVERSE AND AUDIT TYPOLOGY (15:30–21:00)

The audit universe is a concept that CISA candidates must understand both theoretically and practically. The audit universe represents the totality of auditable entities within an organization. It is the comprehensive inventory from which individual audit engagements are selected. This inventory includes business processes, IT systems, applications, infrastructure components, third-party relationships, regulatory compliance obligations, and organizational units. The audit universe is not static — it must be updated periodically to reflect changes in the organization's structure, technology landscape, risk profile, and regulatory environment.

The construction of the audit universe is driven by risk assessment. Not every element in the audit universe will be audited every year. The audit plan — typically an annual plan approved by the audit committee — selects engagements from the audit universe based on risk prioritization. High-risk areas are audited more frequently; lower-risk areas are audited on a rotational basis. This risk-based approach to audit planning is a fundamental principle that ISACA examines repeatedly.

Now let us address audit typology. The CISA exam expects you to distinguish among several types of audits, and questions will present scenarios where you must identify which type of audit is being described.

Compliance audits evaluate whether an organization is adhering to specific laws, regulations, policies, or contractual requirements. A compliance audit does not assess whether the rules themselves are effective — it assesses whether the organization is following them. For example, an audit that evaluates whether an organization's data handling practices conform to the General Data Protection Regulation is a compliance audit.

Operational audits — sometimes called performance audits — evaluate the efficiency and effectiveness of organizational processes. Unlike compliance audits, which ask "are you following the rules," operational audits ask "are your processes working well." An operational audit might examine whether the incident response process resolves incidents within the service level agreement timeframes, or whether the change management process minimizes the introduction of defects into production.

Financial audits examine the accuracy and completeness of financial records and the internal controls that govern financial reporting. While CISA candidates are not expected to be financial audit experts, they must understand the intersection of IT controls and financial reporting. The IT controls that support financial systems — access controls over the general ledger, automated calculations within enterprise resource planning systems, data integrity controls — are directly relevant to financial audit objectives. This intersection is where the IT auditor adds value to the financial audit team.

Forensic audits are conducted to investigate suspected fraud, misconduct, or legal disputes. Forensic audits require specialized skills in evidence preservation, chain of custody, and legal admissibility. The CISA exam may test whether you understand that forensic audits have different evidence handling requirements than routine audits. In a forensic context, the auditor must ensure that evidence is collected in a manner that preserves its integrity and admissibility in legal proceedings.

IS audits — information systems audits — evaluate the controls within information technology environments. This is the CISA candidate's primary domain. IS audits assess general controls, which apply across the IT environment — such as access management, change management, and IT operations — as well as application controls, which are specific to individual applications — such as input validation, processing controls, and output controls. The distinction between general controls and application controls is a perennial exam topic.

Here is an exam trap. ISACA may present a scenario and ask you to identify the type of audit being conducted. Pay close attention to the stated objective of the audit. If the objective is to verify adherence to a regulation, it is a compliance audit — even if the auditor discovers control weaknesses along the way. The type of audit is defined by its objective, not by what the auditor happens to find during execution.

## THE AUDIT CHARTER (21:00–24:30)

The audit charter is the foundational governance document that establishes the internal audit function's authority, responsibility, and accountability within the organization. For the CISA exam, you need to understand both what the charter contains and why it matters.

The audit charter is approved by the highest level of governance — typically the board of directors or its audit committee. This is not a discretionary detail; it is a governance requirement. The charter must be approved at the board level to ensure that the audit function has sufficient organizational independence and authority to perform its work without undue management influence.

The charter should define the following elements. First, the purpose and mission of the internal audit function. Second, the scope of audit activities — which should be unrestricted in terms of access to records, personnel, and physical properties relevant to the audit. Third, the reporting relationship — the chief audit executive should report functionally to the audit committee and administratively to senior management, typically the CEO. This dual reporting structure is designed to preserve independence. Fourth, the charter should establish the audit function's authority to access information, personnel, and assets needed to perform audit work. Fifth, it should define the responsibility for follow-up on audit findings.

Exam trap: the CISA exam will test whether you understand the reporting relationship of the internal audit function. The chief audit executive reports functionally to the audit committee — not to the CIO, not to the CFO, and not to the IT director. Functional reporting to the audit committee is what preserves organizational independence. If a question presents a scenario where the audit director reports to the CIO, you should recognize this as an independence impairment.

Another frequently tested concept: the audit charter should be reviewed and updated periodically to ensure it remains relevant to the organization's current structure and risk environment. It is not a document that is created once and forgotten. Changes in regulatory requirements, organizational structure, or the scope of audit activities may necessitate charter updates.

Let me give you a mnemonic for the key elements of the audit charter: PRSAR — Purpose, Reporting relationship, Scope, Authority, Responsibility. PRSAR. Think of it as "PRofeSSionals AuditoRs" — a bit forced, but the letters stick.

## CISA EXAM STRUCTURE AND SCORING (24:30–29:45)

Let us now turn to the mechanics of the CISA examination itself, because understanding how the exam works is itself a strategic advantage.

The CISA exam consists of one hundred and fifty questions, all multiple-choice, each with four answer options. You have four hours — two hundred and forty minutes — to complete the exam. That gives you ninety-six seconds per question on average. While that may sound generous, many CISA questions present complex scenarios that require careful reading. Time management is not trivial.

The exam covers five domains, weighted as follows. Domain 1, Information Systems Auditing Process, carries twenty-one percent. Domain 2, Governance and Management of IT, carries seventeen percent. Domain 3, Information Systems Acquisition, Development, and Implementation, carries twelve percent. Domain 4, Information Systems Operations and Business Resilience, carries twenty-three percent — making it the heaviest domain on the exam. Domain 5, Protection of Information Assets, carries twenty-seven percent.

Here is a mnemonic for the domain weights in descending order: Domain 5 is heaviest at twenty-seven, then Domain 4 at twenty-three, then Domain 1 at twenty-one, then Domain 2 at seventeen, and Domain 3 is lightest at twelve. Think "5-4-1-2-3" as the weight ranking. The two "protection and operations" domains together account for fifty percent of the exam. If you are going to over-invest study time anywhere, invest it there.

Now, scoring methodology. This is where candidates frequently harbor misconceptions. The CISA exam uses a scaled scoring methodology, not a simple percentage-correct model. Raw scores — the number of questions answered correctly — are converted to a scaled score ranging from two hundred to eight hundred. The passing score is four hundred and fifty. This scaled scoring model accounts for variations in difficulty across different exam forms. What this means practically is that you cannot determine your passing probability by counting "how many did I get right out of one hundred fifty." The scaling algorithm adjusts for question difficulty.

There is an important nuance here. Of the one hundred and fifty questions on the exam, some are unscored pretest questions. These are questions that ISACA is evaluating for potential use in future exams. You will not know which questions are scored and which are pretest items. This means you must treat every question as if it counts — because you cannot tell which ones do not.

Exam trap: candidates sometimes panic when they encounter a question on a topic that seems obscure or unusually difficult. These may be pretest questions that do not count toward your score. The strategic response is to answer every question to the best of your ability, flag uncertain items for review if time permits, and avoid spending excessive time on any single question. A useful rule of thumb: if you have spent more than two minutes on a question and remain uncertain, select your best answer, flag it, and move on. Return to flagged questions after completing the full exam if time permits.

The CISA exam is delivered through PSI testing centers worldwide, and it is also available as a remote-proctored examination. The exam is offered in multiple languages. Results are typically available within ten business days.

Let me address a question candidates frequently ask: what percentage of questions must I answer correctly to pass? Because of the scaled scoring methodology, there is no fixed percentage. However, historical analysis suggests that candidates who correctly answer approximately sixty-five to seventy percent of scored questions will typically achieve a passing scaled score. This is an approximation, not a guarantee, and it varies by exam form. Do not anchor to a specific number. Instead, aim for mastery of the material across all five domains.

## PROFESSIONAL ETHICS AND CODE OF CONDUCT (29:45–34:00)

ISACA's Code of Professional Ethics is not merely aspirational guidance — it is a binding obligation for all CISA holders, and it is directly testable on the exam. Violations of the code can result in revocation of the CISA certification. Let me walk through the key principles.

The ISACA Code of Professional Ethics requires members and certification holders to support the implementation of, and encourage compliance with, appropriate standards and procedures for the effective governance and management of enterprise information systems and technology. It requires them to perform their duties with objectivity, due diligence, and professional care, in accordance with professional standards. It requires them to serve in the interest of stakeholders in a lawful manner, while maintaining high standards of conduct and character, and not engage in acts discreditable to the profession. It requires them to maintain the privacy and confidentiality of information obtained in the course of their activities, unless disclosure is required by legal authority. It requires them to maintain competency in their respective fields. And it requires them to inform appropriate parties of the results of work performed, including the disclosure of all significant facts known to them that, if not disclosed, may distort the reporting of results.

Let me unpack the ethical principles that are most frequently tested. First, confidentiality. An IS auditor who obtains sensitive information during an audit engagement must not disclose that information to unauthorized parties. However — and this is a critical nuance the exam tests — if the auditor discovers evidence of illegal activity, the auditor has an obligation to report that finding through appropriate channels. The duty of confidentiality does not shield criminal conduct. Questions may present a scenario where the auditor discovers fraud and ask whether the auditor should maintain confidentiality. The correct answer is that the auditor should report the finding to appropriate management or the audit committee, even though the information is sensitive.

Second, objectivity and independence. The IS auditor must not have any conflict of interest that could impair objectivity. If an auditor is asked to audit a system that the auditor previously helped design or implement, the auditor has a conflict of interest. The ISACA standard is clear: an IS auditor should not audit their own work. The cooling-off period — the minimum time that must elapse before an auditor can audit an area where they previously had operational responsibility — is typically twelve months, though organizational policy may require a longer period.

Third, competence. The auditor must possess the knowledge, skills, and technical proficiency necessary to perform the assigned audit work. If an auditor is assigned an engagement that requires specialized expertise — say, evaluating the security of a blockchain-based supply chain system — and the auditor lacks that expertise, the appropriate action is to either obtain the necessary training, engage a subject matter expert, or decline the engagement. Proceeding without adequate competence is an ethical violation.

Exam trap: ISACA loves to test scenarios involving independence and objectivity. If a question describes an auditor who was previously the database administrator and is now assigned to audit the database environment, the correct response is that the auditor should disclose the conflict and either recuse themselves or ensure that the conflict is appropriately managed. The auditor should not simply proceed with the audit.

A mnemonic for the core ethical principles: COCCI — Confidentiality, Objectivity, Competence, Conduct (professional), Inform stakeholders. COCCI. Five letters, five principles.

## CONTINUING PROFESSIONAL EDUCATION (34:00–36:15)

Once you earn the CISA certification, maintaining it requires ongoing commitment. ISACA requires CISA holders to earn a minimum of twenty continuing professional education hours per year and a minimum of one hundred and twenty CPE hours over a three-year reporting period. These CPE hours must be in areas relevant to the CISA job practice — information systems auditing, control, security, or governance.

Qualifying CPE activities include attending professional conferences, completing relevant training courses, publishing professional articles or white papers, teaching or lecturing in relevant subject areas, and participating in ISACA chapter activities. Self-study is also a qualifying activity, though ISACA may require documentation to verify self-study hours.

In addition to CPE requirements, CISA holders must pay an annual maintenance fee to ISACA. Failure to meet CPE requirements or pay the maintenance fee can result in suspension or revocation of the certification.

Exam trap: the exam may present a question about the minimum annual CPE requirement. Remember: twenty hours per year, one hundred and twenty over three years. The annual minimum is twenty, not the simple annual average of forty that you might calculate by dividing one hundred and twenty by three. This means you can distribute hours unevenly across the three-year period — perhaps earning fifty hours in one year and thirty-five in each of the other two — as long as no single year falls below twenty and the three-year total meets one hundred and twenty.

A quick mnemonic: twenty is the floor, one-twenty is the door. Twenty hours minimum per year keeps your certification active; one hundred and twenty over three years keeps the door to continued certification open.
